Project Overview

Redesign Menopause Assessment Results Page

Menopause Awareness Month was approaching, and Gennev knew there would be an influx of potential new patients. So we turned our attention to one of our main sources of traffic - the Menopause Assessment.

The Menopause Assessment consistently had a low rate of users converting to patients. In depth user research was in order!

There are 3 main parts to consider when looking at the low conversion rate of users - the assessment questions, the results and the patient signup process. In this case study, I focus on the results page. I redesigned the results page layout, improved the content, and implemented accessibility improvements.

User Testing with the Current Design

Interviewing 10 Menopausal Women to find the pain points

Meet User Persona, Robyn

  • Age: 52
  • Has been in menopause for 12 years
  • Has accepted menopause as a part of life
  • Has visited the doctor for menopause but left with her symptoms untreated

What are Robyn's pain points with the current design?

Lack of Competitive Differentiation

On the results page, there is a lot of text, all justified to the center - Robyn did not want to read through all of this! After a quick skim, Robyn has no idea how Gennev is different form her current doctors. She does not feel enticed to take action when she can recieve the same care from her own doctors.

Inaccessible, Confusing Chart

On the results page, there is a chart that visualizes the userâ€™s menopause type and their symptoms. The chart is difficult to understand and really hard for Robyn to read. The text was way too small and she doesnâ€™t have her glasses with her! The need for a digestible, accessible chart was apparent.

Addressing the Pain Points

Current Design vs. the Final Redesign

Providing Competitive Differentiation

To help women understand what sets Gennev apart, I highlighted how Gennev doctors and care providers specialize in menopause throughout the assessment. I provided data to on how menopause training is uncommon among medical residents to further support the differentiation.

Providing a simple, accessible Menopause Type Meter

I made the chart more accessible by making the font bigger and easier to read. I reduced the amount of information on the chart to make it easier to understand. I divided the information that was on the chart into sections to make it easier to skim and process.

Providing a clear Value Proposition

I highlighted Gennevâ€™s value proposition by listing out what users can expect and the benefits within each of Gennevâ€™s offerings. I formatted this information in a list and used icons to make it easier to read. I included drop-downs with additional information on the benefits, and provided a "Learn More" link.

Takeaways & Highlights

  • Understanding the data is key!
  • Focus on understanding user emotions and then applying emotional design
  • Design content for short attention spans
  • Cover accessibility from the very start - when conducting user research!
